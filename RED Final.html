Part 1: Optimize Pore Radius¶

1a) Model Pressure-Driven Flow through Cylindrical Pore¶

Flow through a cylindrical pipe can be modelled by the Navier-Stokes Equation in cylindrical coordinates with no-slip boundary conditions at the pore surface and symmetry about the central axis.
$$ u(r) = u_{max} \left(1 - \left(\frac{r}{a}\right)^2\right) $$ $$ $$ $$ u_{max} = \frac{\Delta P a^2}{4\mu L} $$

Velocity in the Debye layer will be approximated by a constant: $$ u_{debye} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{\lambda}{a} u_{max}$$

Flow is driven by osmotic pressure, which may be calculated with: $$\Delta P = \pi = \frac{RT}{V}x_{salt}$$

1b) Calculate Debye Length¶

The debye length in the pore is a function of bulk concentration: $$ \lambda = \left(\frac{\epsilon R T}{2 F^2 C_{inf}}\right)^{0.5} $$ $$ $$ The bulk concentration on the interior of the pore is determined by two opposing phemomenon: Diffusion of ions from the saltwater stream to the freshwater stream, and convective transport of ions due to osmotic flow in the opposite direction. Diffusive forces make the concentration in the pore approach Cs (saltwater), while convective forces make the concentration in the pore approach Cf (freshwater)

From scaling theory, calculation of a Peclet number (using the fluid velocity in the center of the pore, length of pore, and diffusivity of NaCl ions in water) indicates that convective flow dominates over diffusion of ions in the opposite direction. $$ $$ $$ Pe = \frac{U_{max} L}{D} >> 1$$ $$ $$ As a result, the bulk concentration in the pore will be dominated by convective forces and will have approximately the same composition as the fresh water stream. Furthermore, the debye length will be approximated as constant throughout the length of the pore.

1c) Model Electric Potential in the Debye Layer¶

Electric potential in the debye layer is governed by Poisson's equation: $$ \epsilon \nabla^2\phi = -\rho $$

The Debye-Huckel approximation gives the resulting chemical potential: $$ \phi = \zeta e^{-x/\lambda} $$

where $\rho$ is the electric charge density and $\zeta$ 'zeta potential' is the electric potential at the shear surface

the charge density within the debye layer is approximated as constant: $$ \rho_{debye} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\sigma_s}{\lambda} $$

1d) Calculate Streaming Current¶

Charge is carried in the debye layer due to convective flow. The current density from this effect is: $$ i_{osm} = \rho u = F z (C_+ - C_-) u $$

The streaming current is therefore given by (with constant approximations for u and $\rho$) $$ $$ $$ I_{st} = \int_{a-\lambda}^{a} \rho u 2 \pi r dr $$ $$ $$ $$ I_{st} = \rho u \int_{a-\lambda}^{a} 2 \pi r dr $$ $$ $$ $$ I_{st} = \rho u \left(a^2 - (a-\lambda)^2\right) $$ $$ $$ And the net streaming current is given by: $$ I = I_{st} n_{pores} $$

Analysis of the effect of pore radius on streaming current¶

The influence of pore radius on streaming current is interesting: Increasing pore radius increases the total volume of fluid in the debye layer (because the inner surface area of the pore increases with a), which increases the streaming current for an individual pore.

At the same time, however, increasing pore size decreases the number of pores and therefore hinders the net streaming current for this membrane. The result is a net streaming current which increases with pore size, but at a decreasing rate (concave down).

1e) Calculate Streaming Potential¶

Streaming Potential is calculated as the stopping voltage of the streaming current. This may be accomplished by setting the net current of the streaming and leak currents to zero: $$ I = I_{st} + I_{leak} = 0 $$

leak current occurs outside of the debye layer and is calculated by: $$ i_{leak} = \frac{-F^2 D}{R T} (C_+ + C_-) \frac{d\phi}{dx} $$

outside of the debye layer, $\rho$ = 0, and thus the leak current density simplifies to: $$ i_{leak} = \frac{-2 F^2 D C }{R T} \frac{d\phi}{dx} $$

Total leak current is therefore: $$ I_{leak} = \int_{0}^{a-\lambda} \frac{-2 F^2 D C }{R T} \frac{d\phi}{dx} 2 \pi r dr $$

Assuming a constant Electric Field, this integral is: $$ I_{leak} = \frac{-2 F^2 D C }{R T} \frac{\Delta\phi_{st}}{L} (a-\lambda)^2 $$ $$ $$ $$ I = I_{st} + I_{leak} = 0 $$ $$ $$ $$ \frac{2 F^2 D C }{R T} \frac{\Delta\phi_{st}}{L} (a-\lambda)^2 = I_{st} $$ $$ $$ $$ \Delta\phi_{st} = I_{st} \frac{R T L}{2 F^2 D C \pi (a-\lambda)^2} $$

Furthermore, the Power generated by this single Reverse Electrodialysis membrane may be calculated by: $$ P_{out} = I_{st} V_{st} n_{pores} $$

Analysis of the effect of pore radius on Power output¶

Similar to streaming current, output power increases with increasing pore size- quicky at first, due to the gain in streaming current flux area, but later dampened by the loss in pore density. Furthermore, as pore radius increases, the necessary stopping voltage (streaming potential) flattens out because all the ions in the pore (not just the excess charge in the debye layer) contribute to the stopping potential. This results in a decreasing marginal gains for the power output as pore radius is increased.

Analysis of the effect of pore radius on efficiency¶

The optimal pore radius is determined to be the debye length.
While increasing the pore radius will always result in a higher power output, these increases are dampened by the loss in pore density and decreasing gains in streaming potential. As a result, the quadratic increase in hydrodynamic power input eventually overtakes the growing power output and creates an optimal pore radius equal to the debye length. At radii larger than the debye length, hydrodynamic power, pore density, and streaming potential limit efficiency, while at radii smaller than the debye length, the full amount of power from streaming potential (excess charge) is not being realized.

The maximum achievable efficiency with this model is 0.906%. This is not a very high efficiency, but the streaming potential is essentially free. The only costs are capital (PPE, membrane R&D, etc.), and pumping. The latter will be analyzed in part 2.

Part 2: Optimize Energy Output: Optimal Channel Width and Flowrate¶

The second part of this project completes the design of an electrodialysis plant by optimizing the flowrate and channel thickness of the two feed streams while minimizing pumping energy costs. The metric for this optimiztion process is (net) Energy output per unit area.

While inexpensive, low flowrates will lead to dilution of the saltwater stream, leading to decreased osmotic pressure and therefore decreased power output. An optimal flowrate will therefore minimize the dilution effects while also minimizing pumping energy costs. Further, decrease in channel width will increase flowrate (and therefore power output, to an extent) but also increase pumping energy costs.

All the preceding calculations have been for a single membrane separating saltwater and freshwater. The following flow calculations will be made for a single channel (of either saltwater or freshwater composition); thus, the overall results are easily scalable.

def Flow(dP):
    Q = dP*np.pi*lam**4/(8*mu*L)     # m^3/s   (Volumetric Flowrate)
    umax = dP*lam**2/(4*mu*L)        # m/s     (maximum fluid velocity)
    u = 0.5*umax                     # m/s     (fluid velocity in debye layer)
    return [u,Q]

2a) Mass Balance¶

Dilution Effects will be analyzed by a mass balance along the length of the column. Only the saltwater stream determines the osmotic pressure, so only the mass balance on the saltwater stream needs to be calculated.

Saltwater stream mass balance: (The saltwater stream experiences mass flow from membranes on both sides, so the flowrate entering from the membranes will be twice the osmotic flowrate calculated in part 1f.) $$ Q_{in} + 2Q_{osm} = Q_{out} $$ $$ Q_{in}C_{in} + 2Q_{osm}C_{osm} = Q_{out}C_{out} $$ $$ $$ $$ C_{in} = C_{salt} $$ $$ C_{osm} = C_{water} $$ Solving for the outflow concentration yields the equation: $$ C_{out} = \frac{Q_{in}C_{in} + 2Q_{osm}C_{osm}}{Q_{out}} $$ This concentration may be used to calculate the Osmotic pressure (and therefore power output) at the end of the column.

These calculations assume an osmotic flow through the membrane which does not vary along the length of the column. The full differential equations could be modeled, however the constant assumption will be valid for flowrates in which the osmotic pressure does not substantially change over the length of the column (<5% decrese) - which is desired in this design. Optimal flowrates will therefore be limited to the domain where exit concentration is approximately greater than 0.95*Cs (0.95M).

# Model Dilution (Mass Balance)
def Mass_Bal(w):  
    # set up vector of inlet flowrates
    t = np.linspace(2,100,1000)           # s  (flowrate timescales, 1 to 60s)
    Qin = Area*w/t
    Cin = Cs                              # moles/m^3 (inlet Concentration is the Salt Stream Concentration)
        
    # calculate osmotic flowrate 
    [u,Q] = Flow(P_osm(Cin))
    Qosm = Q*npores.evalf(subs={a:lam})   # m^3/s  (total flowrate across all pores)
    Cosm = Cf                             # moles/m^3 (osmotic flow is fresh Stream Concentration)

    # Calculate outlet flowrate and Concentration.
    Qout = Qin + 2*Qosm
    Cout = (Qin*Cin + 2*Qosm*Cosm)/Qout

    return [Cout,Qin]

# Find minimum flowrate based on threshold dilution of 5%
def Q_lower(Cout,Qin):
    Cmax = 0.95*Cs
    index = next(x[0] for x in enumerate(Cout) if x[1] < Cmax)
    Qmin = Qin[index]
    
    return index,Qmin

2b) Dilution Effect on Power Output¶

The power output is calculated using the average of the inlet and outlet osmotic pressures. This assumption is valid in the domain where there is minimal dilution effects; the drop in osmotic pressure will be approximately linear across the length of the column for this flowrate domain (0.546 W for this particular setup).

In addition, the lower bound for the flowrate is established at 5% dilution.

# Calculate Reverse Electrodialysis Power output factoring in Dilution effects
def Power(Cout):
    # calculate osmotic pressure at start and end of column
    dPout = P_osm(Cout)
    dPin = P_osm(Cs)

    # Average osmotic pressure over length of column
    dPavg = (dPin + dPout)/2

    # Model Membrane, calculate power
    [u,Q] = Flow(dPavg)  
    Ist = Ist_u(u,lam)   
    Vst = Vst_Ist(Ist,lam)
    Pout = Ist*Vst*npores.evalf(subs={a:lam})
    
    return Pout

2c) Pumping Energy Cost, Energy Generation Comparison¶

The energy to pump fluid through the apparatus is; $$ E_{pump} = Q_{in} \Delta P_{pump} $$ $$ $$ pressure drop may be calculated using the Reynolds number and Darcy friction factor, for turbulent flow: $$ D_h = \frac{4A}{P} $$ $$ $$ $$ Re = \frac{\rho u D_{h}}{\mu} $$ $$ $$ $$ f = 0.079 Re^{-0.25} $$ $$ $$ $$ \Delta P_{pump} = \frac{2 f L \rho u^2}{D_h} $$

In [711]:
def Pump(Qin):
    v = Qin/(w*l)            # m/s    (fluid velocity)
    p = 1000                 # kg/m^3 (density of water)
  
    A = w*l                  # m^2    (channel area)
    P = 2*(w+l)              # m      (channel perimiter)
    Dh = 4*A/P               # m      (Hydraulic Diameter- in this case, approaches 2*w)
    
    Re = p*v*Dh/mu           #        (Reynold's Number)
 
    f = 0.079*Re**(-0.25)    #        (friction factor)

    dPump = f*l*2*p*v**2/Dh  # Pa     (Pressure Drop)

    E = dPump*Qin            # W      (Pumping Energy)
    return E

E_pump = Pump(Qin)

def E_area(w,index,Pout,E_pump):

Conclusion¶

This project investigated the design of a Reverse Electrodialysis power generation plant:

in Part 1, Pore size was optimized such that the maximum amount of hydrodynamic power was extracted as energy. The optimal pore size was determined to be the debye length. The maximum attainable efficiency was determined to be 0.91%

in Part 2, the net power output per square meter was optimized, resulting in an optimal flowrate and channel width which maximized efficiency of pumping energy to power RED energy generation. The optimal channel width was determined to be 4.7 mm and flowrate to be 93.7 L/s per cell (1 cell = 182 channels, 1m^3). These conditions produce a Reverse Electrodialysis power generation plant which is infinitely scalable and which produces 76 Watts per square memter of footprint.

Analysis of scale shows that despite RED being infinitely scalable, it still requires a very large footprint to compete with other sources of energy generation. Ultimately, though osmotic pressure is a promising source of power, RED has low efficiencies. Furthermore, it is only really applicable in areas where freshwater and saltwater contact, such as the emptying of a river into the sea.

Further analysis may be made by modelling the full differential equations for fluid flow along the length of the column to investigate flow regimes which have higher dilution but may produce higher Energy per unit area.
